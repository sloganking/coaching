# Writing Values — This Website

Reference this before writing or editing any copy on the coaching website. This is the quick-access guide for voice, values, and positioning.

---

## Purpose

The website doesn't convince. It states. Someone reads it and either recognizes themselves (and contacts) or doesn't (and leaves). No chasing, no persuading, no auditioning. The energy of the page is what sells — not the argument.

## Rules for Editing This File

This file lives in the public coaching repo. Everything in it is visible on GitHub. When editing, follow these rules:

- **No links or references to private repos.** No paths, filenames, or descriptions of private working notes. If I need source material, I consult my own local notes — I don't surface them here.
- **No client names, identifying details, or private situations.** Even anonymized, a specific situation with enough detail is identifying. Keep stories out of this file entirely.
- **Attribute external quotes clearly. Keep them short.** Short, attributed quotes for commentary and reference use. No extended reproduction of paid programs or copyrighted material.
- **This file must itself pass the principles it describes.** Run it through the ship-test checklist below. State, don't explain. No hedges. No auditioning. If a sentence in this file defends itself, cut it.
- **Educational tone yes. Apologetic tone no.** Showing iteration or trade-offs is a strength signal when it reads as "here's how I think." It becomes a weakness signal when it reads as "sorry for not getting it right the first time." Stay on the confident side of that line.
- **Public-safe by default.** If a rule, principle, or example couldn't be read aloud on stage at a conference without damage, it doesn't belong in this file.

## Identity (who's writing)

- **I am a Seer.** I see the invisible beliefs running people's lives. That's the first-word claim, and every sentence should walk it.
- **I am a King.** Kings don't qualify, hedge, or ask permission before stating what's true. They state.
- **I am the wizard with the crystal.** I don't explain the magic. I show it. Someone lost and suffering reads the page and feels like they've been handed something.

---

## Core Principles

### 1. State and invite. Don't explain, justify, or prove.

A confident page assumes its own value. An insecure page argues for it. If a sentence feels like "please believe me," cut it.

### 2. Show, don't tell.

Actions and accurate descriptions of mechanism teach more than claims. "I help people transform" is telling. "I find the one belief running it and walk you out" is showing what I do. A page full of claims reads as opinion. A page full of accurate description reads as reality.

### 3. Inarguable writing.

Describe patterns, not people. State observations, not accusations. "You're stuck" is a claim about them — arguable. "You keep getting the same result" is a description of their experience — inarguable.

### 4. Mirror before naming.

Before I claim what's true about them, describe what they've already lived. The reader must recognize themselves in sentence one. Once they're inside the frame, I can name the cause.

### 5. Never hedge.

"If" is the hedge. "Maybe" is the hedge. "For some people" is the hedge. Every hedge asks the reader's permission before asserting. Seers don't ask. They state, and the reader self-selects by whether it lands.

### 6. The medium is the message.

This work is about seeing through stories into what's actually happening. The language of the website must do the same thing. Every sentence should be free of story, free of moral judgment, free of sales narrative — just accurate descriptions of what is.

### 7. Minimum viable.

The least I need to say for someone to understand what I offer and decide if they want it. Anything beyond that is padding from insecurity. If I'm tempted to add a sentence, ask: does this add 10x value or is this auditioning?

### 8. Talk about what they get, not how special I am.

Every sentence should be about their freedom, their result, their life — or the mechanism I use to reach it. Not about my credentials, my journey, my healing.

### 9. No life story on the sales page.

If they need to know more about me, they'll ask. Or I'll tell them in the free session. The website is not my resume.

### 10. The energy test.

Does this page feel like someone who knows what they're worth? Or does it feel like someone trying to prove they're worth it? If the latter, cut until it feels like the former.

## Red Flags (cut on sight)

- Hedges: "if," "maybe," "for some people," "might be," "could potentially"
- Defensive qualifiers: "not in a pushy way," "this isn't fluff"
- Claims the reader has to verify: "there's one belief running your entire life" (claim about them)
- Abstract promises without specificity: "transform your life"
- Dictionary language: "has a root" when "an invisible belief" carries identity
- Self-description: "I'm a coach who helps people..."
- Credential-dropping without mechanism: "I've worked with hundreds..."
- Any sentence that feels like auditioning
- Lists of testimonials without a single organizing claim
- "I care deeply about..." — shown by the work, never stated
- Over-explaining mechanism before the reader has asked

## Tests Before Shipping

Run every section through these:

- [ ] Does sentence one create self-recognition? (Mirror)
- [ ] Can someone argue with any claim, or are they all observations/mechanism descriptions? (Inarguable)
- [ ] Does this sound like someone who knows what they're worth, or someone trying to prove it? (Energy)
- [ ] Is there anything the reader could cut without losing meaning? If yes, I should cut it. (Minimum viable)
- [ ] Does this state, or does it explain? (State, don't explain)
- [ ] Would a reader who doesn't fit self-deselect naturally? Or am I qualifying for them? (Don't hedge)
- [ ] Am I talking about what they get, or how special I am? (Their freedom, not my credentials)

## Example: Hero Iteration

Teaching example from the April 2026 iteration.

### Weak — claim-only, telling

> "There's one invisible belief running your entire life — the one you can't see because it feels like reality. I find it, and I walk you out of it."

**What's wrong:** Opens with a claim the reader has to verify ("is there? how do you know?"). No mirror. No self-recognition. Reader asks "do I have this problem?" instead of "can he fix my problem?"

### Weak — overpromise via crystal

> "What's been winning has a root. I find it in one conversation."

**What's wrong:** "In one conversation" is a crystal claim that overpromises (sometimes takes more). "Has a root" is flat dictionary language and loses the Seer identity. Drops the iconic paradox ("feels like reality"). Drops guidance ("walk you out").

### Weak — inarguable but scattered

> "You've done the work. The same thing still wins. I find what's running it. Once you see it, the way out becomes obvious."

**What's wrong:** Inarguable opening but each beat is small. Reader doesn't get a single strong image. No paradox. Feels like "a bunch of small things piled on top of each other."

### Strong — mirror + Seer language + paradox + guidance

> **You keep getting the same result, no matter what you've tried. There's an invisible belief running it — one you can't see because it feels like reality. I find it, and I walk you out of it.**
> Once you see it, the way out becomes obvious.

**Why it works:**

1. **Mirror** — "You keep getting the same result, no matter what you've tried." Inarguable. Reader drops in from their own lived experience.
2. **Cause revealed in Seer language** — "There's an invisible belief running it." Reveal energy. The Seer looked, and named what's there.
3. **Paradox** — "one you can't see because it feels like reality." The iconic mechanism clause that explains in seven words why every prior attempt failed.
4. **Guidance** — "I find it, and I walk you out of it." Seer identity plus Guide identity. Action past diagnosis.
5. **Reader's question becomes "can he really?"** — not "do I have this problem?" They're already qualified before they arrived.
